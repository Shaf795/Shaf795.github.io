Teaching Philosophy

My approach to teaching mechanical engineering emphasizes conceptual clarity, physical interpretation, and numerical validation. Students are encouraged to understand not only the mathematical formulation but also the mechanical behavior behind equations and simulation results.

The goal is to bridge theory and computational practice, enabling learners to confidently analyze real engineering problems.

Core Subjects

Stress Analysis

  • Stress and strain fundamentals
  • Elasticity and constitutive relations
  • Principal stresses and Mohr’s circle
  • Failure theories
  • Physical interpretation of stress fields

Finite Element Method (FEM)

  • Weak form and element formulation
  • 1D, 2D, and axisymmetric elements
  • Meshing strategies
  • Convergence and validation
  • Post-processing and result interpretation

Fracture Mechanics (LEFM)

  • Stress intensity factors (SIF)
  • Modes I, II, and mixed-mode loading
  • Crack tip fields
  • Energy release rate concepts
  • Numerical modeling of cracks

Experimental Stress Analysis (Photoelasticity)

  • Principle of stress-induced birefringence
  • Isochromatic and isoclinic fringes
  • Circular polariscope setup
  • Qualitative and quantitative stress interpretation
  • Validation of analytical and FEM results

Photoelasticity & Circular Polariscope

Photoelasticity is a powerful experimental technique used to visualize stress fields and stress concentration effects in transparent models. A circular polariscope setup enables clear observation of isochromatic fringe patterns, which represent contours of constant principal stress difference.

Integrated Learning: Theory, Simulation, and Experiment

Learning is structured to integrate theoretical foundations with numerical simulation and experimental observation. Students and researchers are guided in implementing solid mechanics and fracture mechanics concepts using finite element tools such as ANSYS APDL, ABAQUS (with Python scripting), and MATLAB.

Equal emphasis is placed on correct modeling assumptions, mesh refinement near stress singularities, extraction and interpretation of stress intensity factors, and validation of numerical results using experimental stress analysis techniques such as photoelasticity with circular polariscope setups.

Who Can Benefit from This Platform

  • Undergraduate students developing strong fundamentals in stress analysis and solid mechanics
  • Postgraduate students learning finite element methods and computational analysis
  • PhD scholars working on fracture mechanics and structural integrity problems
  • Researchers and industry professionals revisiting core mechanics concepts with numerical and experimental insight
